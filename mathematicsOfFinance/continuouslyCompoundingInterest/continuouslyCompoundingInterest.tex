\title{Section 5.3 Interest Compounded Continuously}
\author{Conner Griffin}

\begin{document}
\begin{abstract}
    In many cases, interest compounds ``continuously'' meaning that interest is being applied constantly rather than periodically. We present a formula for continuously compounding interest and a corresponding present value formula.
\end{abstract}
\maketitle

As \(n\) goes towards infinity, \(\left(1+\frac{1}{n}\right)^n\) gets closer and closer to \(e,\) Euler's constant. Interest that compounds with annual frequency \(k\) at annual rate \(r\) is calculated with the formula,

\[S= P\left(1+\frac{r}{k}\right)^{kt}\]

where \(kt\) is the number of interest periods that go by.

If interest is compounding continuously, then we are letting the annual compounding frequency go to infinity.

Rewriting our formula,

\[P\left(1+\frac{r}{k}\right)^{kt} = P\left(\left(1+\frac{r}{k}\right)^{\frac{k}{r}}\right)^{rt} = P\left(\left(1+\frac{1}{k/r}\right)^{\frac{k}{r}}\right)^{rt}.\]

Letting \(n=\frac{k}{r}.\) We note that \(n\) is going to infinity with \(k\) and that

\[P\left(\left(1+\frac{1}{k/r}\right)^{\frac{k}{r}}\right)^{rt}=P\left(\left(1+\frac{1}{n}\right)^{n}\right)^{rt}\]

is going to 

\[Pe^{rt}.\]
