SPECIAL TYPES OF MATRICES

A \emph{square matrix} is a matrix of size \(n \times n\) for some natural number \(n.\) That is the matrix has the same number of rows and columns.

\begin{example}
    \[\begin{bmatrix}
        1 & 7 & 4 \\
        3 & 1 & 2 \\
        8 & 5 & 1
    \end{bmatrix}_{3\times 3}\]
    is a square matrix since it has \(3\) rows and \(3\) columns.
\end{example}

The \emph{\(n \times n\) identity matrix} is the matrix with \(1\)s along ``the diagonal'' and \(0\)s everywhere else. It is denoted \(I_{n \times n}.\)

The \emph{diagonal} (sometimes called the main diagonal) are the \(i,i\) entries.

\begin{example}
    \(\begin{bmatrix}
        1 & 0 \\
        0 & 1
    \end{bmatrix}\) is the \(2\times 2\) identity matrix.

    \(\begin{bmatrix}
        1 & 0 & 0\\
        0 & 1 & 0\\
        0 & 0 & 1
    \end{bmatrix}\) is the \(3\times 3\) identity matrix.

        \(\begin{bmatrix}
        0 & 0 & 1\\
        0 & 1 & 0\\
        1 & 0 & 0
    \end{bmatrix}\) is \emph{not} the \(3\times 3\) identity matrix. The \(1\)s are along a diagonal but they are not along the \emph{main} diagonal.
\end{example}

A \emph{diagonal matrix} is a square matrix whose entries off of the diagonal are all zero.

\begin{example}
    \(\begin{bmatrix}
        2 & 0 & 0 \\
        0 & 5 & 0 \\
        0 & 0 & 1
    \end{bmatrix}\) is a \(3 \times 3\) diagonal matrix.

    \(\begin{bmatrix}
        5 & 0 & 0\\
        0 & 1 & 0 \\
        0 & 0 & 0
    \end{bmatrix}\) is a \(3 \times 3\) diagonal matrix.
\end{example}
